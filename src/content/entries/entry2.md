During the winter months Brother Karl lost his life. He was ill and wasting. We buried him underneath a tree by his yellow home. 
A few years ago, a woman and her baby died in childbirth, and many winters ago–right around when we first arrived, a family died from their house collapsing on top of them. There were four of them, I recall. 

I have not yet slaughtered a sheep. I have only ever killed fish.

When Brother Karl died I was brought along to help clean the space since he was my father’s elder sibling. We took the linens and gave them to Augusta and Reginold– the elderly couple by the chapel. The kitchen items went to the chapel’s collection. The clothing was given to my Pa and I. Pa found Karl’s sermon supplies and gave them to Reverend Thomas. He did not take everything though. I found a blank book buried in a box full of damaged, old clothes and linens in the back of Karl’s closet. When I saw that book I felt the weight of Lucet’s eyes upon me. Lucet knows all things including what you will do throughout your entire life. She is surprised by nothing and disappointed by all. I knew the storm of trouble I would be into if Pa or Reverend Thomas found out what I had done. The book was neither mine to take nor an object I would be cleared to have. Blank books are for clergy.

I took it anyway.
I don’t know why I took that book, but I don’t have to know why because Lucet knows for me.

My closet has two shelves. On the highest shelf my summer clothes and hat are stored. I hid the journal underneath my straw hat and left it for weeks. I was too afraid to take it out from there, I was too afraid of Lucet’s gaze to bring myself to touch it. I contemplated returning it, but I couldn’t manage to find the courage to do what was right. Eventually, I relented and I quietly snuck it down, under my arm, and to the rocky, little cove that’s under the bluff. Josef was there waiting for me and skipping stones.  
I sat on the log that we dragged down there some years ago and stared at him down by the kinetic shore. I made my confession to him. He asked if I knew what I wanted to put in it and I did not know. He then asked me why I took it. I did not know; I simply wanted it. 
Josef told me that since it was in possession that I should put it to proper use.

When Josef and I were still of schooling age we were taught about proper use. I recall that we wanted to fold sailboats to play in the ocean with, so we stuffed some leaflets from the classroom with us into the folds of our clothing. Eventually, we were found out and forced to answer to Reverend Thomas, who said that we were giving in to our sinful natures and told us that he did not approve of tomfoolery with commune property. He said it could have been used for something better. He hit our hands with canes and made us assume extra chores around the commune: Josef and I had to chop firewood.Those leaflets could have been used for learning. This blank journal was likely to be used by Karl to help manage the commune with Reverend Thomas. 

Whatever I decided to write within this book must not be a waste.
