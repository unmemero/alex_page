Reverend Thomas delivered an impassioned sermon this evening. He said that our time, which has been spent suffering and in pain, will all be forgotten soon. We have been on this miserable plot for almost a decade, and as God proclaimed during the tenth winter Lucet’s holy light will clean our slate and bring us to our knees- repenting- and soon we will be nothing. 

It is only speeches with him. 

Josef rose from his pew that he sat at with his parents and bravely offered a bespoke objection to Brother Thomas’s words. Josef asked, perhaps rather he implored, all listening, why were we the only ones to lay down and die before our actual demise. He asked us to consider the town and all of the boats– why they haven’t bent to God’s will. If we are to die anyways, he questioned, why do we subject ourselves to conditions worse than our bleak futures. 
Reverend Thomas’s face swelled with God’s wrath. He reminded Josef and the commune, harshly, that he received God’s final will because he is the only one that is able to lead us through temptation’s brambles. Those that we departed from and those that we are neighbors to follow falsehoods that displease God and Lucet; by our suffering and removal from society we appease God’s original will and prevent ourselves from slipping farther and farther into toil. 
Brother Thomas demanded that Josef come to his lectern. He caned Josef’s hands and then caned his spine. Josef did not flinch to this infliction, but I knew his heart burned more than his flesh. Josef returned to his pew, and Brother Thomas prohibited any person from the beach and spoke more of punishment after death.  
After he delivered his word, the word of God, Josef and I traversed out into the knolls with the sheep. I asked him to come along; I could no longer bear the thought of nothingness, of being so alone, so I cultivated for myself something to have even if for a short while. 
I find it absurd that Brother Thomas and the rest of the commune can accept our demise. Has he never been cooled by the ocean or felt pleasure in enjoying sausages, biscuits, and gravy? I implored Josef with my questions. 
Josef, just as impassioned if not more than Brother Thomas, spoke very seriously. His eyes were hot as coals. He told me that when we all die, whether or not Lucet will bring us to our demise or not, we will face nothingness. 
This I already knew. I told him again that I, too, find it absurd that there are others, those in the ships and those in the town, that continue to bring humanity forward instead of pulling back to wait for our final days. 
It is unfounded, he said, that we sit here twiddling our thumbs waiting for Lucet when society- real society, he exclaimed, has and continues to build on. He then revealed to me perhaps his biggest sin: he said he goes to the town, sometimes, at night.

My initial judgements were infectious. I felt betrayed, but I allowed this feeling to subside before I demanded answers. 
Josef assured me that he only ever went to the outskirts of town, but had still discovered so much . He said that what is there is much like from where we came, but these people think bigger and have no concept of an earlier demise. They think in the present and they think of the future as something that must be built. It is integral that we go and live amongst those with no idea of Lucet. He said if I wanted to live I must join him and that he would give me a while to think. 
I asked him how he proposed to even do such a thing, where we would go, how we would make a living? It was then when he revealed to me that while in town, he asked around in search of work and a man informed him of a relatively new outpost further up North that needed extra working hands.  He said that the base could be found by looking for the yellow houses- like ours- sitting atop Magnolia Bluff. It could be a way out, he said, but we have to be brave enough to leave this all behind.
I told him that I did not want to die and if our neighbors to the North do not want to die either, then we are surely destined to be something.
There is something within my rotten soul that wants to be something- that wants to live, to be alive. I want to put forth instead of recoil. There are songs that have been living within me since before we took the long trail to Washington. I have been writing them out, but that is not enough. There has to be more.
Is it better to die- lay down and accept our sorry fate, I asked Josef. Is it worth trying to spend our final days passing our time passively? 
Josef rested his bruised hand on my shoulder, he had the same soft look within his eyes. I felt heavy with weight. I sat upon the grass and tossed my staff to the floor. He joined me. We sat there for a while listening to the sheep bleat. 
We returned to the shepherd shed. We did not speak. The sheep got penned and my staff was hung up. We lingered a while longer. 
There is a song within me that cannot go unplayed, I realize that now. It gets louder by the day- more clear too.  
Josef asked me if I wanted to play it. I did, more than anything. 
He said he supposed it would be better to try and play it. But what is there to do, I pleaded. He said that I knew, but I had to be the one to say it. He couldn’t be the one to lead me astray. 
We must leave, right? We must take nothing that is not ours, and leave in the night with a Northern mission to the town. We could follow the creek upwards, Josef said. Once there, we would take it day by day until the winter. We will die amongst the sinners and sailors, surrounded by rubble, and then we will return to God.
