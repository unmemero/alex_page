Sometimes I try to count the stars. I find Her first, then count the stars that encircle Her. 

Before I began to count the stars I was always very scared. I never knew when the sky would go black or white, or blue, or purple. I used to be unnerved by the unequal numbers of stars in the sky. Some nights I would count two hundred fifty three and other times two hundred fifty eight. Sometimes I would lose track by sixty and stare at her glow and wonder.
I remember when we first began to follow Her: I was very young, and I couldn’t quite see her as I do now. When we were still pilgrimaging I could never quite pick her out of the sky even with the point of my Pa’s finger. Instead of locating Her under the layers of cloud and the depths of the sky, my gaze would always wander to the moon. 
I can pick her out of the sky now regardless of time or location. I’ve made sure of it because her fixation in the sky often leads me back home. When the sheep have eaten through all of the good grass, we have to lead them further into the knolls. If our group that year is particularly large we will have to lead them further and further– into the woods that cling onto a sandy bluff. It is a terrible job to do during the winter when the night sets in early and the rain and wind is brought from the bitter ocean. My final step to working as a shepherd required I assume the evening, winter shift. When I’d lose track of my surroundings, and the night clouds covered the sky too thickly to see Her in the sky, her presence, which was almost as visible on Earth as it was in the Heavens, always led me back home. 

It will be spring soon, and we will need to shear the sheep. I love to shepherd; I tend to my sheep like a mother would rear a child. Their care is my priority. When guarding their pasture I do not count stars for long- only for short moments to keep me awake. 
My sheep are good creatures and they serve us well. They supply meat, milk, wool, and leather. They die as they should- in service. It gets harder to withdraw my care for them especially during the shearing season. Lester and Horace tie, drag, and throw them. They beat them and kick them when they walk astray. I want to stop them; I want to hurt them back, but that will not do. I hold my tongue and let my fingernails dig into my palm. I cannot appear too attached and risk the evil eye of the commune. I do not wish to sit through Reverend Thomas’ speeches or when he hits my knuckles with the cane. 

When Lucet revealed herself to me, during the fourth Winter, I felt that everything that could ever be known would be known through Her- through brutal winters, harsh conditions, and solitude. She blessed me with the knowledge that Reverend Thomas used to guide us here. I understand that our suffering now is to assume the sin of our past, but that no amount of suffering can atone for the debts we have earned. When it is time for Her to call us back, we will have to pay more than we paid on Earth.  
I do not know what I have done. I am the unfortunate son to misfortune as is everyone else. We were born too late to plead for forgiveness, and now we have to atone. 

Josef once asked me if I had seen anyone else, other than our commune, on the long trail to Washington. I had to look deep within my mind to remember, but I did. I asked him why they were not with us and he shrugged. He told me to consider. I asked if they had died or if they had been lost. He did not affirm. I asked if they stopped believing and watched his eyes sort of soften. He said that they did not believe at all. 

There are people who do not believe in Lucet at all.
