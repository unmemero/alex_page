Just the other day another vessel passed by our settlement. We’ve been seeing more boats than we did when we first arrived. They’re always large enough to see without having to stand by the beach or on the edge of the bluff. I wondered how many people were on these boats. I wonder if any of them were guided here by Lucet. 
I ran into Brother Thomas from the shepherd’s shed to my home. I bid him a good morning and noted the presence of the ship. He appeared grave when he told me that we are possibly the only people that have heard God’s true will. He insisted that it was our top property to resist contact with these disobedient folk. 

When the boats first began arriving everyone was on high alert for months, Reverend Thomas often guided everyone into the chapel to discuss what we had seen. 
He affirmed that there were always boats in the bay and told us that he does not know the purpose that boat has or whether or not it has ties to the production of the town that’s further north. He reminded us that the ways of the cityfolk, from where we first came, were misaligned with our original purpose. 

I could tell that Reverend Thomas was a storm in a bottle. I quickly got home and to my room. I took out the journal and fingered through the pages that I’ve already written and those that still needed ink to fill them. I am enjoying my progression; I am enjoying putting the songs in my head on the page. Each day they become clearer and clearer.
I can't quite shake the image of the boat upon the water and all of its contents and people. We must be very blessed to be the only community to have heard God’s will. We were guided resolutely with Lucet’s guiding light; I can’t imagine what it is these pilgrims are led by. A boat that big must carry a lot of drifting souls. I wonder who guides and leads them. I wonder how big that town must have gotten.
	
—

The ocean spray mists my face and the salty wind makes my hair clump against my head. I am standing on the deck right by the ship’s outstretched arm. If I put my hand on my shoulder and trace my outstretched arm down to my index finger I can see what the boat sees: wide, blue waters. I see land. It is rocky and mountainous. Further down the bay there will be docks lined up one after the other in a long row. Just beyond that is the town. When I get off of this boat I hope to shake the hand of everyone I see.
